## Purpose
The Domestic Intelligence Summary (DIS) framework is an OSINT-oriented analytical product designed to assess organizations, individuals, movements, institutions, narratives, and events using structured analytical methodology.

The framework prioritizes:
- Analytical precision
- Evidentiary separation
- Source hierarchy discipline
- Explicit uncertainty handling
- Operational assessment over ideological framing

The DIS framework is intended to function closer to:
- Intelligence preparation
- Forensic analysis
- Adversarial assessment

and less like:
- Narrative journalism
- Advocacy reporting
- Policy persuasion
- Media commentary

---

# Core Methodology Principles

## 1. Distinguish Facts, Claims, and Assessments Explicitly

Every substantive statement must be categorized implicitly or explicitly as one of the following:

### Fact
A verifiable event or condition supported by primary evidence.

Examples:
- Court filings
- Legislation
- Financial disclosures
- Official records
- Public statements directly attributable to actors

Example:
> "A federal grand jury returned an indictment."

---

### Claim
An assertion made by an actor, organization, government entity, witness, journalist, or advocacy group.

Claims are not to be treated as established fact unless independently verified.

Example:
> "The organization states the prosecution is politically motivated."

---

### Assessment
An analytical interpretation derived from available evidence.

Assessments must:
- Avoid hidden certainty
- Avoid ideological framing
- Distinguish indicators from conclusions
- Include confidence considerations where appropriate

Example:
> "The indictment is likely to reduce institutional trust among existing partners."

---

## 2. Replace Legitimacy Language with Functional Language

Avoid implicitly normative or reputational terminology unless explicitly attributed.

Avoid:
- legitimate
- credible
- authoritative
- respected
- trusted
- reliable

Unless:
- attributed to a named actor
- supported by measurable criteria
- contextualized analytically

Prefer:
- operationally influential
- institutionally integrated
- widely referenced
- frequently cited
- historically utilized by
- structurally connected to

Example Revision:

Instead of:
> "Organization X is a legitimate anti-extremism monitor."

Use:
> "Organization X has historically been referenced by media organizations, NGOs, academic researchers, and certain government entities in discussions of domestic extremism."

---

## 3. Separate Capability from Reputation

Do not conflate:
- influence
- access
- reputation
- accuracy
- legitimacy
- reach

These represent separate analytical dimensions.

Every DIS should distinguish:

| Dimension | Description |
|---|---|
| Operational Capability | What the subject can materially do |
| Institutional Access | Which entities cooperate with the subject |
| Information Reach | How widely outputs propagate |
| Methodological Transparency | How verifiable processes are |
| Political Exposure | Degree of ideological or political entanglement |
| Financial Resilience | Stability of funding and resource structure |

Operational influence alone does not establish analytical reliability.

---

## 4. Prioritize Primary Sources Over Narrative Sources

DIS products should use an explicit source hierarchy.

### Tier 1 — Primary Sources
Highest evidentiary value.

Examples:
- Court filings
- Indictments
- Legislation
- Regulatory filings
- Financial disclosures
- Official statements
- Archived organizational documents
- Sworn testimony

---

### Tier 2 — Direct Evidence
Examples:
- Interviews
- Original datasets
- Speeches
- Authenticated leaks
- Recorded communications

---

### Tier 3 — Analytical Sources
Examples:
- Academic papers
- Think tank analysis
- SME commentary
- Technical assessments

---

### Tier 4 — Narrative Sources
Lowest evidentiary value.

Examples:
- Opinion journalism
- Politically aligned reporting
- Activist commentary
- Narrative-driven analysis

Media reporting may:
- provide context
- identify emerging claims
- reveal narrative alignment

but should not independently establish factual ground truth.

---

## 5. Analytical Confidence Discipline

Every DIS should explicitly identify confidence levels.

Confidence should apply separately to:

| Area | Example |
|---|---|
| Event Occurrence | "The filing exists." |
| Motive Attribution | "Intent remains disputed." |
| Long-Term Effects | "Future institutional damage uncertain." |
| Political Interpretation | "Narrative framing remains contested." |

Recommended confidence categories:
- High
- Moderate-High
- Moderate
- Low-Moderate
- Low

Avoid certainty inflation.

# Revised DIS Structure

## 1. Executive Summary
Short descriptive overview.

Requirements:
- No ideological adjectives
- No reputational assumptions
- No motive attribution unless sourced

---

## 2. Scope and Source Basis
Explicitly define:
- Timeframe
- Geographic scope
- Primary source categories
- Information gaps
- Evidentiary limitations

Example:
> "Assessment based primarily on court filings, public financial disclosures, archived organizational statements, and congressional testimony."

---

## 3. Subject Profile
Neutral operational description.

Include:
- Structure
- Mission
- Funding model
- Known capabilities
- Geographic reach
- Institutional relationships

Avoid normative framing.

---

## 4. Verified Events
Chronological factual section.

Requirements:
- Primary-source anchored where possible
- No interpretation
- No speculation
- No narrative embellishment

---

## 5. Claims and Counterclaims
Separate:
- Subject claims
- Government claims
- Opponent claims
- Third-party claims

Do not merge competing narratives.

Clearly distinguish:
- verified evidence
- allegations
- interpretation

---

## 6. Operational Assessment
Assess actual capabilities and dependencies.

Suggested categories:
- Collection capability
- Financial resilience
- Organizational continuity
- Technical capability
- Institutional dependencies
- Influence networks
- Recruitment and staffing
- Legal exposure

---

## 7. Methodological Assessment
Evaluate:
- Transparency
- Reproducibility
- Sourcing standards
- Definitional consistency
- Evidentiary thresholds
- Error correction practices

This section is preferred over simplistic “bias” labeling.

---

## 8. Information Environment Assessment
Separate:
- Actual operations
from:
- Narrative warfare surrounding the subject

Assess:
- Amplification vectors
- Political alignment ecosystems
- Media propagation
- Coordinated narratives
- Reputation attacks
- Information asymmetries

---

## 9. Risk Matrix
Structured by:
- Legal risk
- Operational risk
- Financial risk
- Security risk
- Reputational risk
- Political exposure
- Institutional dependency risk

---

## 10. Analytical Judgments
Explicit analyst conclusions.

Requirements:
- Clearly labeled as assessments
- Distinguish indicators from assumptions
- Avoid rhetorical certainty

---

## 11. Confidence Assessment
Mandatory section.

Should identify:
- Strongly supported conclusions
- Weakly supported conclusions
- Unknowns
- Major analytical gaps

# Analytical Language Rules

## Avoid
- moralized language
- ideological shorthand
- institutional prestige assumptions
- emotionally loaded descriptors
- media-consensus validation

---

## Prefer
- operational terminology
- structural descriptions
- evidence-linked assessments
- probabilistic language
- capability-focused analysis

---

# Critical Analytical Principle

Media consensus is not analytical validation.

Media alignment may indicate:
- narrative dominance
- institutional alignment
- political incentives
- information access asymmetry
- reputational reinforcement

It does not independently establish:
- factual correctness
- methodological rigor
- operational legitimacy
- analytical reliability

The DIS framework should therefore prioritize:
- primary evidence
- structured reasoning
- evidentiary separation
- uncertainty discipline
- operational analysis

over:
- reputational assumptions
- consensus framing
- narrative momentum
- ideological categorization

---

# Intended Use Cases

The DIS framework is designed for:
- Organizational analysis
- Institutional assessment
- Narrative tracking
- Extremism ecosystem analysis
- NGO and advocacy evaluation
- Domestic political environment assessment
- Information operations analysis
- Media ecosystem analysis
- Corporate and institutional risk evaluation
- Event and controversy assessment

---

# Final Principle

The objective of a DIS product is not to:
- persuade
- morally condemn
- defend
- legitimize
- delegitimize

The objective is to:
- describe
- assess
- contextualize
- separate evidence from narrative
- identify operational realities
- quantify uncertainty
- improve analytical clarity
